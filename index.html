🔄 ADPIE — The nursing process
A — Assessment
Gather subjective (patient reports) and objective (observed) data. Verify accuracy. Types: Initial/Baseline, Focused, Emergency (ABCs), Ongoing/Reassessment.
D — Diagnosis
Identify problem → form NANDA-approved nursing diagnosis. Actual (current problem) vs Potential (at risk for). NO action taken here — only problems identified.
P — Planning
Set SMART goals: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound. Goals must be patient-centered and measurable.
I — Implementation
Put plan into action = NURSING INTERVENTIONS. e.g. placing SCDs, giving medications, educating patient.
E — Evaluation
Determine outcomes of goals. Evaluate effectiveness, modify and assess for needed changes to plan.
Subjective vs Objective data
Subjective = what patient reports ("6/10 pain"). Objective = what nurse observes (auscultating bowel sounds, vital signs). Remember O for Objective and Observation.

Medical vs Surgical Asepsis
Medical = clean technique (reduces pathogens) — used for meds, tube feeds, hygiene. Surgical = sterile technique (eliminates all pathogens) — used for dressing changes, catheters, surgery.
⚠️ Patient safety
Fall precautions: Fall assessment q shift · Items in reach · Bed alarm on + call bell in reach · Hourly rounding
Seizure precautions: Pads on side rails · Suction at bedside · Bed in low position · O₂ set up
Suicide precautions: 1:1 sitter at ALL times · Remove all potential harm objects (cords, curtains, silverware, belts, mirrors, sharps) · Encourage discussion of feelings
Restraints: LAST RESORT only · Requires MD order · Release and reassess every 2 hours (non-violent) · 4 side rails up = restraint requiring MD order
🔥 Fire safety — RACE & PASS
RACE: R — Rescue patients · A — Activate alarm · C — Confine fire · E — Extinguish
PASS (extinguisher): P — Pull the pin · A — Aim at base · S — Squeeze handle · S — Sweep side to side
Sharp injury — needle stick: ① Stop & clean with soap/water · ② Notify charge nurse · ③ Blood drawn for HIV/Hepatitis testing · ④ Prophylaxis may be recommended if source positive
Safe patient handling: Never twist at waist · Never move patient alone · Use mechanical lifts · Never recap needles — always use safety lock
📊 Prioritization framework
ABCs first
Always address Airway → Breathing → Circulation first. Then Safety → Discomfort → Education → Feelings.
Stable vs Unstable
Prioritize patients actively deteriorating from baseline. Symptomatic HR 120 patient takes priority over asymptomatic HR 120.
Expected vs Unexpected
Prioritize unexpected symptoms. Post-op nausea is expected; post-op nausea + abdominal pain is unexpected → higher priority.
Actual vs Potential
Current problems take priority over potential risks. Patient with current hypotension over patient at risk for hypotension.
Acute vs Chronic
Acute problems take priority over chronic. Patient with new hypotension (baseline 120/80) over chronically hypotensive patient on milrinone.
Maslow\'s Hierarchy
Physiological needs (ABCs = O₂, food, homeostasis) → Safety → Love/Belonging → Self-Esteem → Self-Actualization. ABCs are PHYSIOLOGICAL = most important.
📊 Normal vital sign ranges
O₂ saturation
Normal: 94–100%. Low = Hypoxia. Note: COPD patients may naturally present lower.
Temperature
Normal: 97.8–99°F (36.5–37.2°C). <95°F = Hypothermia. >104°F = Hyperthermia/fever.
Respirations
Normal: 12–20 breaths/min. <12 = Bradypnea. >20 = Tachypnea.
Pulse
Normal: 60–100 bpm. >100 = Tachycardia. <60 = Bradycardia.
Blood pressure
Normal: <120/<80. Hypotensive: <90 systolic/<60 diastolic. Stage 2 HTN: ≥140/≥90.
Pain scales
Numerical (0-10, patient rates). Faces scale (pictures). PAINAD (dementia patients — 5 behaviors). CPOT (critically ill/intubated — objective factors).
Early hypoxia signs
Restlessness, anxiety, tachycardia, tachypnea. These come FIRST — act now.
Late hypoxia signs
Bradycardia, extreme restlessness, dyspnea, hypotension, cyanosis. EMERGENCY.
Cheyne-Stokes breathing
Deep/rapid breathing followed by apnea. Often seen at end of life.
Kussmaul breathing
Fast, deep breathing in response to metabolic acidosis (e.g. DKA).

🧠 Head-to-toe assessment overview
Assessment order: IPPA
1) Inspect (visual) · 2) Palpate (touch) · 3) Percuss (tap/sound) · 4) Auscultate (stethoscope). Exception: Abdomen = IAPA (auscultate BEFORE palpating to avoid false bowel sounds).
Orientation (AAO)
AAOx1 = person only. AAOx2 = person + place OR time. AAOx3 = person + place + time. AAOx4 = person + place + time + situation. Ask: current year or president (not day of week).
Level of consciousness
Awake → Confused → Disoriented → Lethargic → Obtunded → Stuporous → Comatose. From least to most impaired.
Cranial Nerve 7 (Facial)
Raise eyebrows · Smile · Frown · Show teeth · Puff out cheeks · Squeeze eyes shut. Tests facial nerve symmetry.
PERRLA
Pupils Equal Round Reactive to Light and Accommodation. Assessed during eye/neuro check.
Urine color guide
Clear = overhydration · Pale yellow = normal · Amber = dehydrated · Brown = liver disease · Pink/red = blood, food dye, kidney disease · Green = medication or bacterial infection
Capillary refill
Normal <2–3 seconds. Press finger, count time to regain color. >3 seconds = poor perfusion.
Bowel sounds
Hyperactive >30/min · Normoactive 5–30/min · Hypoactive <5/min · Absent = must listen 5 minutes before declaring absent.
Heart auscultation — APETM
All People Enjoy Time Magazine. Aortic → Pulmonic → Erb point → Tricuspid → Mitral (apex). Listen for murmurs, muffled, or whooshing sounds.
Pitting edema grading
+1 = 2mm trace · +2 = 4mm mild · +3 = 6mm moderate · +4 = 8mm severe. Check legs, ankles, feet.
Pulse scale
0 = absent · 1+ = weak · 2+ = normal · 3+ = increased · 4+ = bounding. Start at pedal pulse, move up to femoral.
Skin color abnormalities
Pallor = pale (low blood flow) · Jaundice = yellow/orange (liver/bilirubin) · Erythema = red/pink (inflammation) · Cyanosis = blue (low O₂). Always check mucous membranes in dark-skinned patients.

⚡ Electrolyte imbalances — signs & mnemonics
Hypernatremia (>145)
Mnemonic: FRIED SALT — Flushed/fever, Restless/irritable, Increased BP/fluid retention, Edema, Decreased urine output/dry mouth, Skin flushed, Agitation, Low grade fever, Thirst. Na & K are OPPOSITES: ↑Na = ↓K+
Hyponatremia (<135)
Mnemonic: SALT LOSS — Stupor/coma, Anorexia/N/V, Lethargy, Tendon reflexes decreased, Limp muscles, Orthostatic hypotension, Seizures/headache, Stomach cramping. Treat based on cause.
Hyperkalemia (>5)
Mnemonic: MURDER — Muscle cramps, Urine (oliguria/anuria), Respiratory distress, Decreased cardiac contractility, EKG changes (PEAKED T WAVES), Reflexes decreased. NEVER give K+ IV push → cardiac arrest!
Hypokalemia (<3.5)
Mnemonic: 6 L's — Lethargy, Leg cramps, Limp muscles, Low shallow respirations, Lethal cardiac dysrhythmias, Lots of urine. EKG: T wave inversion. Monitor Mg & Ca (often linked).
Hypercalcemia (>11)
Mnemonic: BACKME — Bone pain, Arrhythmias, Cardiac arrest, Kidney stones, Muscle weakness, Excessive urination. Ca & Phosphate are OPPOSITES: ↑Ca = ↓P.
Hypocalcemia (<9)
Mnemonic: CATS GO NUMB — Convulsions, Arrhythmias, Tetany, Stridor/spasms, GO NUMB (numbness in face/fingers/limbs). Trousseau\'s sign (hand spasm with BP cuff) + Chvostek\'s sign (facial spasm with facial nerve tap).
Hypermagnesemia (>2.5)
Think everything LOW: Low energy/drowsy, Low HR (brady), Low BP (hypotension), Low RR (bradypnea), Low DTR (hyporefexia), Low bowel sounds. Calcium gluconate is antidote.
Hypomagnesemia (<1.5)
Think everything HIGH: High HR (tachy), High BP, High DTR (hyperrefexia), High RR (fast/shallow). HYPERACTIVE state + irritability, seizures, twitches. NEVER give IV Mg fast — give SLOW.
💊 6 Rights of medication administration
Right Patient
2 patient identifiers before every medication. Name + DOB (or MRN). NEVER room number.
Right Time
Give within 30 minutes before or after scheduled time (unless STAT or time-sensitive).
Right Dose
Verify calculation. Leading zeros required (0.5 mg NOT .5 mg). No trailing zeros (5 mg NOT 5.0 mg).
Right Medication
Verify 3 times: when removing from storage, when preparing, and when administering.
Right Route
Verify the route ordered matches how you are giving it (PO, IV, IM, SubQ, topical, etc.).
Right Documentation
Document immediately after giving. Never document before giving.

Dosage calculation formulas
Tablet/pill dose
D/H × Q = A. (Dose ordered ÷ Amount on hand) × Quantity = Amount needed. e.g. 400mg ordered, 200mg tablets: 400/200 × 1 = 2 tablets
Injectable dose
D/H × V = A. (Dose ordered ÷ Amount on hand) × Volume = Amount needed. e.g. 20mg ordered, 100mg/10mL: 20/100 × 10 = 2 mL
IV flow rate (pump)
V ÷ T = mL/hour. Volume (mL) ÷ Time (hours) = rate. e.g. NS 1000mL over 8 hrs: 1000/8 = 125 mL/hr
IV flow rate (gravity/drops)
(V ÷ T) × drop factor = gtt/min. Convert hours to minutes first. Round gtt to whole number.
Weight-based dose
D/H × kg = A. Convert lbs to kg first (÷ 2.2). e.g. 4mg/kg ordered, patient 130 lbs (59.1 kg): 4mg × 59.1 = 236.4 mg/dose
Rounding rules
Number >1: round to nearest tenth. Number <1: round to nearest hundredth. Capsules: whole number only. Tablets: nearest half. IV drip: whole number (gtt).
💉 IV fluid types
HYPERTONIC — cells shrink
Higher solute concentration than blood. Water leaves cells. Examples: 3% saline, 5% saline, D5NS, D5LR, D10W. ANYTHING ABOVE 0.9% is hypertonic. Uses: hyponatremia, cerebral edema, hypovolemia. Monitor for hypervolemia. High concentrations need central line (extravasation risk).
ISOTONIC — cells stay same
Same concentration as blood. Cells do not shrink or swell. Examples: 0.9% NS, Lactated Ringers (LR), D5W (starts isotonic, becomes hypotonic when metabolized). Uses: blood loss, shock, dehydration. NS is ONLY fluid used with blood products.
HYPOTONIC — cells swell
Lower solute than blood. Water enters cells. Examples: 0.45% NS (1/2 NS), 0.33% NS, 0.225% NS. ANYTHING BELOW 0.9%. Uses: cellular dehydration, DKA, hypernatremia. Risk: hyponatremia or hemolysis (cells swell and burst).
IV complications
Phlebitis: Inflammation of vein. S/S: pain, redness, tenderness, decreased flow. Treatment: remove IV, warm compress, monitor.
Infiltration: IV fluid leaks into tissue. S/S: edema, pallor, pain, cool skin. Treatment: remove IV, elevate extremity, compress.
Extravasation: Vesicant drug leaks — can cause blisters, tissue sloughing, necrosis. Vasopressors, chemo agents, D10 are vesicants.
Infection: Local or systemic from IV site. S/S: redness, fever, chills. Treatment: remove IV, blood cultures, antibiotics.
Hematoma: Blood in tissue. S/S: hard lump, bruising, pain. Treatment: remove IV, hold pressure, elevate.
Blood transfusion nursing steps
① Verify patency of IV line
② Verify consent signed
③ Verify blood product + patient identity with 2nd RN
④ Full set of vitals PRIOR to starting
⑤ Spike blood on tubing, start infusion SLOW
⑥ STAY with patient for first 15 minutes
⑦ Reassess vitals at 15 min mark
⑧ Monitor vitals every 30 min–1 hr during infusion

⚠️ Only NS can be used with blood. Must start within 30 min of picking up. Transfuse within 4 hours of starting.
Transfusion reactions — stop transfusion IMMEDIATELY for any reaction
Hemolytic
Immune system kills donor RBCs. S/S: low back pain, chills, tachycardia.
Febrile
Recipient RBCs react to donor RBCs. S/S: temp increase 1°C from baseline, flushing, chills.
Allergic
Immune reaction to plasma proteins. S/S: flushing, rash/hives, itching.
Septic
Contaminated blood transfused. S/S: rapid onset of chills, high fever, tachycardia.
Circulatory overload
Too rapid transfusion. S/S: dyspnea, crackles or rales, JVD.
Nursing actions for ANY reaction
① Stop transfusion IMMEDIATELY · ② Change IV tubing + run NS wide open · ③ Notify physician & blood bank · ④ Have emergency medications ready
🩹 Pressure injury stages
Stage 1
Intact skin. Non-blanchable redness. Area may be swollen and irritated. Skin unbroken.
Stage 2
Skin NOT intact. Shallow open ulcer (partial thickness loss). Open/closed blister. NO exposed fat.
Stage 3
Full thickness skin loss. SubQ fat MAY be visible. NO exposed bone, muscle, or tendon.
Stage 4
Full thickness skin loss. Exposed bone, tendon, and/or muscle tissue. Tunneling or pockets.
Unstageable
Full thickness with ulcer covered by eschar/slough. MUST be removed first to stage. Risk of evolving to Stage 3 or 4.
DTI (Deep Tissue Injury)
Purple or maroon skin OR blood-filled blister. Non-blanchable, often looks bruise-like. High risk of evolving.
Braden Scale
19–23: no risk · 15–18: mild risk · 13–14: moderate risk · 10–12: high risk. 6 categories: sensory perception, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, friction/shear.
Wound drainage types
Serous = clear & thin · Sanguineous = bloody · Serosanguineous = mix of blood & serous · Purulent = thick, yellow/green, pus-like (infection)
Patient positioning
Supine (flat on back)
Uses: rest, abdominal/chest procedures, physical assessment. Monitor for skin breakdown at sacrum/heels.
Fowler's (semi-sitting)
HOB 45–60°. HOB for tube feeds/vents MUST be ≥30° to prevent aspiration. Low Fowler's 15–30° reduces back pain.
Lateral (side-lying)
Pillow between legs for comfort. Relieves pressure from sacrum and heels.
Prone (on abdomen)
Used for ARDS patients. Promotes drainage after mouth/neck procedures.
Trendelenburg
Head down, feet up. Used for placing central lines and lower abdominal surgeries. NOT recommended for acute hypotension anymore.
Reverse Trendelenburg
Feet down, head up. Reduces esophageal reflux, promotes gastric emptying, lung expansion in obese patients.
🏥 Perioperative care
Preoperative phase
Decision for surgery → transfer to OR table. Nurse focus: support, teaching, procedure prep. Admission assessment, check allergies, VS, consent signed, void before surgery.
Intraoperative phase
OR table → PACU admission. TIME OUT must happen before every procedure: right patient, right procedure, right site, right markings. Circulating nurse = NOT sterile. Scrub nurse = STERILE.
Postoperative phase
PACU admission → follow-up. Close 1:1 monitoring. Q1–Q15 min assessments: VS, respiratory effort, LOC, muscle strength, airway. Monitor pain, assess/manage, educate on discharge.
Pain management — OPQRST
Onset (when did it begin?) · Provoking/Palliating (what brings it on/stops it?) · Quality (what does it feel like?) · Radiation (does it spread?) · Severity (pain scale) · Time (how long?)
Non-opioid pain meds
Acetaminophen, NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin). WHO ladder Step 1: non-opioid + adjuvant for mild pain (1–3).
Opioid pain meds
Morphine, Dilaudid, Fentanyl, Oxycodone. WHO ladder Step 3: strong opioid + non-opioid + adjuvant for severe pain (7–10).
Therapeutic communication
AVOID: false reassurance, leading questions, "why" questions, giving advice. USE: open-ended questions, restating, paraphrasing, reflecting. 93% of communication is NON-VERBAL.
Ethics — 7 principles
Beneficence (do good) · Nonmaleficence (do no harm) · Autonomy (patient right to decide) · Veracity (truth-telling) · Justice (equal care) · Fidelity (keep promises) · Advocacy (support patient decisions)
🍽️ Nutrition & elimination
Enteral vs Parenteral nutrition
Enteral = through GI tract (NG, PEG, J-tube, NJ tube). Higher risk of aspiration. Parenteral = IV (PPN or TPN). Higher risk of sepsis than enteral. TPN must go through central line.
Tube feeding — prevent aspiration
Keep HOB ≥30–45°. Check residual q4h. If >400mL stop feeds and notify provider. Flush tube 20–60mL water to prevent clogging. Use x-ray to verify initial placement.
Stoma colors
Red-pink = NORMAL · Pale pink = ANEMIA · Purple-blue = ISCHEMIA (tissue death — contact provider immediately!)
Urinary catheter care
Always keep drainage bag BELOW bladder. Check for kinks. Clean site daily with mild soap/water. Remove ASAP to reduce CAUTI risk. Always sterile insertion technique.
Constipation danger
Valsalva maneuver (straining) stimulates vagus nerve → slows HR. In high-risk patients can cause brain injury, bradycardia, or cardiac arrest. Prevent with fiber, fluids, activity.
Ostomy output by type
Ileostomy = liquid output. Ascending colostomy = liquid. Transverse colostomy = semi-formed. Descending/sigmoid colostomy = solid & formed.
🌡️ Thermoregulation & ambulation
Heat exhaustion vs heat stroke
Heat exhaustion: headache, cool/pale/clammy skin, excessive sweating, rapid WEAK pulse, fainting. Heat stroke: THROBBING headache, skin hot and DRY (no sweating), rapid STRONG pulse — MEDICAL EMERGENCY call 911.
Malignant hyperthermia
Severe reaction to anesthesia → dangerously high temp. Due to inherited gene mutation. ONLY effective treatment: DANTROLENE. Notify anesthesia immediately.
Crutch stairs mnemonic
GOOD = UP, BAD = DOWN. Going UP stairs: good leg first, then bad leg. Going DOWN stairs: bad leg first, then good leg.
Cane technique
Hold cane on STRONG side. Place 6–10 inches forward. Move cane and weak leg together, then strong leg. Nurse stands on patient WEAK side during ambulation.
Walker gait sequence
① Lift + move walker forward · ② Move weak leg · ③ Put weight on hands · ④ Move strong leg. All 4 walker points must touch ground.
Oxygen delivery devices
Nasal cannula: 1–6 L/min, 24–44% FiO₂. Simple face mask: 6–10 L/min, 35–60%. Venturi mask: 4–12 L/min, 24–60% (most precise). Non-rebreather: 10–15 L/min, up to 90%.
